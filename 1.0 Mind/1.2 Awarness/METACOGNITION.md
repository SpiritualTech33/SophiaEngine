# Metacognition

## What is Metacognition?

Metacognition is a rare phenomenon, but well documented. It is one of those things you rarely know you possess, or are even aware exists — but once you become conscious of it, there is no going back. It is like waking up from a dream you participated in your entire life. Suddenly you are no longer asleep, and once awake, the dream ceases to exist.

Metacognition seems simple at first, even irrelevant — but it hides within itself an immense power for those who begin to practice it consistently.

By this point you are probably asking — what is it?

But before we get to what it is, let us look at the origins of the word itself.

-----

### Metacognition — Etymological Definition

The word is composed of a combination of Greek and Latin roots.

**Meta** — from the Greek, meaning “above” or “beyond.” A prefix that places itself above its subject — you can see it in Metaphysics, Metadata, Metamorphosis.

**Cognition** — inherited from the Latin *Cognoscere*, which means to know, to perceive, and to understand.

So we could say that Metacognition is literally *knowing about knowing.* The capacity to observe your own mind from above it — not as the actor, but as the witness. It is a deeply philosophical concept, one that neuroscience has only recently begun to study seriously, and one that contemplative traditions have practiced for thousands of years.

-----

## Is it a skill?

Yes and no, at the same time. It is within you from the moment you are born, but you have to train it. You have to develop the ability to watch your own mind. It is not always comfortable — there are things in there you may not want to see. But the ability to watch yourself without flinching is another level entirely.

There is a word I love — **equanimous** — the capacity to be unalterable. Nothing from the exterior disturbs your center, because your peace comes from within. That is precisely the ability you begin to unlock when you start observing yourself honestly. To understand yourself, to educate yourself, to improve yourself.

What would happen if your emotions were suddenly no longer the ones guiding you? If your thoughts were no longer the ones deciding what you do? You would no longer identify with your body, your mood, or your impulses. You would identify with something deeper — with presence itself, with love, with light. That is what we are in essence. But to ignite that light, you first have to see it. And to see it, you have to stop confusing it with everything surrounding it.

-----

## Is it hard to learn?

The observation itself is not hard — it is innate. Everyone can self-observe. The difficulty lies elsewhere: in observing without immediately identifying with what you observe.

We habitually believe we are our emotions and thoughts. When we feel anger, we say “I am angry” — not “there is anger present.” That small grammatical difference reflects an enormous psychological one. Everything we experience as “I” or “Self” is largely a neurobiochemical process — shaped by hours of sleep, what we have recently eaten, the activities we perform, and the habits we carry. Our sense of a fixed self is more fluid than we think.

The most effective way to train Metacognition is through meditation. Since I began practicing Zen-style meditation, I have experienced a clarity and capacity for self-knowledge that no other practice has given me. When you sit in silence with your eyes closed, accompanied only by your thoughts, you force your inner observer to be the one in charge. You are no longer reacting. You are watching.

Meditation is the laboratory where Metacognition is developed.

## How it Unlocks Cognitive Powers

Here is where it gets genuinely fascinating — how Metacognition relates to what I call cognitive freedom.

It is a strange state. It could even be considered mystical, because you feel total clarity over everything you consider reality. Not control in the sense of domination — but the kind of clarity that comes from being fully awake inside your own experience. You stop reacting to life and begin responding to it. Every action becomes intentional. Every moment becomes inhabited.

This is what neuroscience calls deep work and what psychology calls the Flow State.

The Flow State is something magical and slightly paradoxical — you do not realize you are inside it until you come out. Time dissolves. The boundary between you and what you are doing disappears. You stop being the body doing the thing and become the thing being done.

It is like right now, as I write these words — I am the letters, I am the voice in your head, I am the moment, because I am writing it.

That is what living the present moment actually means. Not a philosophy. Not a concept. A direct experience available to anyone willing to sit with themselves long enough to find it.

Metacognition is the door. Meditation is the key. The room on the other side is yours.
